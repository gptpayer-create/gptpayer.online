JPG to PDF: The Complete Guide — Best Practices & Settings

Published: April 20, 2026  ·  10 min read  ·  GPTPayer Team

Converting JPG images to PDF is one of the most common digital tasks today. Whether you need to share a certificate, send an invoice, submit documents to a government portal, or compile multiple photos into a single file — JPG to PDF conversion is the go-to solution. But many people do it wrong and end up with blurry output, wrong page sizes, or files that are rejected by official portals.

This guide covers everything you need to know — from choosing the right page size and orientation, to understanding image quality, file size, and common mistakes to avoid.

What Is the Difference Between JPG and PDF?

JPG (also written as JPEG) is an image format primarily designed for photographs and raster graphics. It uses lossy compression, which means some image data is permanently discarded when the file is saved — though this quality loss is usually invisible to the naked eye at standard settings. JPG files are small, widely supported, and ideal for photographs.

PDF (Portable Document Format) is a document format developed by Adobe that can contain text, images, vector graphics, and even interactive elements. The biggest advantage of PDF is that it looks exactly the same on every device, every operating system, and every screen size. Fonts, layouts, and images are all preserved perfectly. This is why governments, banks, universities, and companies prefer PDF for official document exchange.

Why Convert JPG to PDF?

There are many practical reasons why converting a JPG to PDF is better than sharing the image directly:

  • Submitting official documents: Government portals, colleges, and banks almost always require documents in PDF format. Many upload forms simply reject JPG files outright.
  • Combining multiple images: Sending 10 separate JPG files in an email is messy and unprofessional. One PDF document handles all of them cleanly in the correct order.
  • Printing consistency: PDF files behave consistently across different printers. A JPG may print at an unexpected size, but a properly set PDF will print exactly as intended.
  • Preventing edits: A PDF is harder to accidentally modify than a JPG. When you share a certificate or invoice as a PDF, the recipient sees it exactly as you intended.
  • File organization: A single PDF with 20 scanned pages is far easier to manage, archive, and share than 20 separate image files.
  • Professional appearance: PDF attachments look more polished and professional than a collection of image files, especially for business communication.

Which Page Size Should You Choose?

The right page size depends entirely on where your PDF will be used and who will receive it. Choosing the wrong page size can result in your image being cropped, stretched, or surrounded by unexpected white borders.

  • A4 (210 × 297 mm): The global standard used across Europe, Asia, Africa, and most of the world. Best for office documents, certificates, government forms, and most official submissions. When in doubt, use A4.
  • Letter (8.5 × 11 inch): The standard in the United States and Canada. Use this when sending documents to US-based companies, universities, or institutions.
  • A3 (297 × 420 mm): Twice the size of A4. Best for large diagrams, architectural drawings, posters, or wide-format content that needs detail.
  • Custom size: If your image has an unusual aspect ratio — like a receipt, a long form, or a panoramic photo — using a custom page size ensures nothing gets cropped.

Portrait vs. Landscape Orientation

Orientation refers to whether the page is taller than it is wide (Portrait) or wider than it is tall (Landscape). Choosing correctly prevents your image from being rotated, squeezed, or cropped.

Choose Portrait (vertical) when your images are taller than they are wide. This includes ID cards, certificates, receipts, scanned A4 documents, and most standard photos taken in a vertical orientation on your phone.

Choose Landscape (horizontal) when your images are wider than they are tall. This includes panoramic photos, group pictures, spreadsheet screenshots, presentation slides, and any image captured in a horizontal orientation.

⚠️ Common Mistake: Many people upload a landscape-oriented photo but leave the page set to Portrait. The result is a tiny image surrounded by blank space, or a cropped image with the sides cut off. Always match the orientation to your image.

How Much Margin Should You Set?

Margins define the white space between your image and the edge of the PDF page. The right margin depends on how the PDF will be used.

  • For printing: Always set a margin of at least 10–15mm. Most home and office printers cannot print right to the paper edge — the print head needs a minimum unprintable border. If you set zero margin, part of your image may be cut off when printed.
  • For digital submission: A small margin of 5–10mm looks clean and professional even on screen.
  • For full-bleed display: If the PDF is only for screen viewing and you want the image to fill the entire page, set margin to zero.

Image Quality and Resolution

One of the most important things to understand about JPG to PDF conversion is this: the sharpness of your output PDF depends entirely on the quality and resolution of your original JPG. Conversion changes the file format — it does not enhance or improve image quality.

If your source image is blurry, low-resolution, or heavily compressed, the PDF will be blurry too. Always start with the highest quality image you have available. For document scanning, aim for at least 300 DPI (dots per inch) — this is the standard for readable text in scanned documents. For photos, 150–200 DPI is usually sufficient for screen viewing, but print-quality photos should be 300 DPI or higher.

Managing File Size

The file size of your output PDF is roughly proportional to the total size of your input JPG files. A single high-resolution JPG of 5MB will produce a PDF of approximately 5–6MB. If you are combining 20 such images, the resulting PDF could be 100MB or more — too large for most email attachments.

To manage file size before converting, consider resizing your images to a lower resolution if they will only be viewed on screen, or compressing the JPGs before uploading. There is no point in using a 10MB photo when you only need a 1MB document scan.

💡 File Size Tip: Most email providers limit attachments to 25MB and many government portals accept files under 5MB. If your PDF is too large, try reducing the image resolution before converting, or split the content across multiple PDFs.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using screenshots instead of original files: Screenshots are often low resolution and compressed. Always use the original image file for better quality.
  • Ignoring orientation: Uploading a horizontal image to a vertical page template — or vice versa — wastes space and looks unprofessional.
  • Forgetting to check order: When combining multiple images, always review the order before converting. Rearranging pages in an already-created PDF is more difficult.
  • Converting unnecessarily large files: A 20MB photo scanned at 600 DPI is unnecessary for a simple document. Reduce resolution before converting.
  • Using wrong page size: Sending a Letter-sized PDF to a European institution (which expects A4) can cause formatting issues when printing.
