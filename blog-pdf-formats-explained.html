PDF/A, PDF/X, PDF/E — The Different PDF Standards Explained

Published: April 3, 2026  ·  5 min read  ·  GPTPayer Team

Most people think of PDF as a single, simple format — but in reality, PDF has several specialized variants, each designed for a specific industry or use case. If you submit documents to government agencies, work in the printing industry, or handle engineering files, understanding these variants can save you from costly mistakes.

Standard PDF (PDF 1.x / PDF 2.0)

This is the everyday PDF format that most of us use. It supports interactive elements such as forms, hyperlinks, embedded audio and video, and JavaScript. It can be encrypted and is viewable in any modern browser or PDF reader. There are no special restrictions on content. This is the right format for most personal and business use.

PDF/A — Designed for Long-Term Archiving

The "A" in PDF/A stands for Archive. This is an ISO standard (ISO 19005) specifically designed to ensure that a document will still be readable and look identical in 50 or even 100 years, regardless of what software exists at that time. To guarantee this, PDF/A has strict requirements:

  • All fonts must be embedded within the file — no reliance on system fonts.
  • Color spaces must be fully specified.
  • Encryption is not permitted.
  • External content such as videos, audio, and hyperlinks to external resources are not allowed.
  • JavaScript is prohibited.

Government agencies, law firms, banks, and medical institutions use PDF/A to ensure that records remain accessible and unchanged indefinitely. If a government portal requests "PDF/A format," this is what they mean.

PDF/X — Built for the Printing Industry

The "X" in PDF/X stands for Exchange. This standard was developed for the graphic arts and commercial printing industry to ensure that files print exactly as intended when sent from a designer to a print shop. PDF/X enforces CMYK color spaces (what printers use, as opposed to RGB used on screens), requires fonts to be embedded, and mandates that bleed areas and trim marks are properly defined. If you are submitting files to a printing press or publisher, they will likely request PDF/X-1a or PDF/X-4.

PDF/E — Developed for Engineering

The "E" in PDF/E stands for Engineering. This format supports 3D content, interactive CAD drawings, and complex technical documentation. It is used for engineering blueprints, manufacturing designs, product schematics, and construction documents. PDF/E allows for rich interactive 3D models to be embedded directly in the document.

PDF/UA — For Accessibility

The "UA" stands for Universal Accessibility. PDF/UA is designed to make PDF documents fully accessible to people with disabilities, particularly those using screen readers. It requires proper document structure, tagged content, and meaningful reading order. Government websites and public-sector organizations are increasingly required to provide PDF/UA-compliant documents.

Which Format Do You Need?

  • Everyday use — sharing, home printing, general business: Standard PDF
  • Government submissions, legal records, financial documents: PDF/A
  • Commercial printing, design agency submissions: PDF/X
  • CAD drawings, engineering blueprints: PDF/E
  • Public-facing documents on websites: PDF/UA
